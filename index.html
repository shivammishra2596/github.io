How I see the world

"What is yours will find your way,
given you keep trying."
01
Persistence over perfection
The best outcomes I've witnessed — in deals, in strategy, in life — didn't come to the most talented person in the room. They came to the one who kept showing up, kept refining, kept going. Relentlessness is underrated.
02
Data is the beginning, not the answer
Numbers tell you what happened. Asking the right question tells you why. The most dangerous thing in a deal room is someone who's very confident about a very shallow model. I believe in going three levels deeper.
03
Stillness is a strategy
The best thinking I've done wasn't at a desk. It was on a houseboat in Alleppey, watching time dissolve into backwater. Slowing down is how I come back sharper. Rest isn't laziness — it's maintenance.

Travel stories

Some places stay
inside you.

I travel slow. No itinerary army, no landmark checklist. Just a vague direction and the discipline to stop when something feels worth staying for. Kerala was one of those places that never let me leave — at least not completely.

Kerala

Kochi → Munnar → Alleppey → Varkala

The thing about Kerala is — it doesn't try to impress you. It just exists, unapologetically lush and unhurried, and somewhere between the first cup of chai in Fort Kochi and the last sunset at Varkala cliff, it quietly takes a piece of you. You don't notice it happening. That's the point.

I started in Kochi, where the city hasn't quite decided which century it belongs to. Old Portuguese buildings lean into narrow lanes lined with antique dealers and art galleries. The Chinese fishing nets stand against the evening sky like a postcard from a hundred years ago — except they're real, and someone's still using them. I walked through Jew Town on a slow afternoon, fingers trailing along sacks overflowing with cardamom, star anise, and turmeric the colour of a warning label. This is what history smells like, I thought. Warm and slightly overwhelming.

"I had the best cup of tea of my life in a tiny shop with plastic chairs and a fogged-up window."

Then Munnar happened, and I was completely unprepared. Three-hour drives on hairpin bends where clouds literally sit at your window level. You stop talking. The tea gardens stretch in every direction, impossibly green against the grey mist, as if someone spilled an entire watercolour set on the hills and called it done. The cold settles in your chest and makes you want to stay. Indefinitely. I had the best cup of tea of my life in a tiny roadside shop with plastic chairs and a fogged-up window, and I wouldn't trade it for anything in any airport lounge.

Alleppey is what poets write about when they haven't found the right words yet. The backwaters are still in a way that feels almost sacred. On the houseboat, time did something strange — it slowed down, then stopped caring about itself. Paddy fields floated past the edges of the boat. Birds landed on railings, looked at you sideways, and left without explanation. A man on the bank waved at us for absolutely no reason. We waved back. That was the whole interaction. It was enough.

"Some places don't give you clarity. They give you peace. And sometimes that's more than enough."

And then Varkala — perched on a cliff above the Arabian Sea, with cafes playing Bob Marley and serving banana pancakes while the waves crash 30 metres below. It's a little chaotic, a little touristy, unashamedly so. But the sunsets are non-negotiable. Sitting there with my feet hanging off the cliff edge, watching the sky turn gold, then tangerine, then deep indigo, I realised something: some places don't give you clarity. They give you peace. And sometimes — after enough decks, enough deal rooms, enough early morning calls — that's exactly what you needed to find.
